Insights & Legal Updates

Land Ownership in Uganda

Uganda's Land Act (Cap 227) recognizes four land tenure systems: Customary, Freehold, Mailo, and Leasehold.

üìå Customary Tenure: Based on traditional practices, not formally registered, but now eligible for certificates of customary ownership.
üìå Freehold Tenure: Allows full rights to use and dispose of land. Owners can obtain freehold titles.
üìå Mailo Tenure: Unique to Uganda, often includes overlapping rights of landowners and lawful occupants ("bibanja holders").
üìå Leasehold: Land is leased for a fixed period (often 49 or 99 years), with specified terms and conditions.

Marriage Rights in Uganda

The Marriage Act (Cap 251) recognizes four main types of marriage: Civil, Customary, Christian, and Islamic.

‚úÖ Civil Marriage: Conducted by the Registrar of Marriages. Monogamous and governed by statutory law.
‚úÖ Customary Marriage: Based on traditions of specific communities. Must be registered for legal protection.
‚úÖ Christian Marriage: Must occur in a licensed church and registered under the law.
‚úÖ Islamic Marriage: Governed by Sharia and recognized when registered.

All legally recognized marriages protect property, inheritance, and spousal rights, especially when registered.

Tenants‚Äô Rights in Uganda

Under the Landlord and Tenant Act, 2022, both landlords and tenants have protected legal rights.

üè† Tenants: Have the right to written tenancy agreements, privacy, timely repairs, and protection from unlawful eviction.
üõë Landlords: Must give 60-day notice for rent increase, cannot evict without court process, and must provide habitable conditions.

Security deposit handling, rent receipts, and dispute resolution through the tribunal are all outlined in the Act.
